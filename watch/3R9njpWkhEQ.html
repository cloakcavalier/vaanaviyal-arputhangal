There may now be concrete evidence that a planet is not necessarily extinguished when its host star dies. Astronomers have spotted signs of an intact giant planet circling a dead star, known as a white dwarf, a new report by scientists at the University of Wisconsin reveals. The white dwarf, called WS1856, is part of a star system approximately 80 light-years from Earth. The newly detected “survivor” exoplanet, called WD1856b, is about seven times bigger than the white dwarf, which it circles it once every 34 hours, the report states. It’s about the same size as Jupiter and has managed to stay in one piece. It’s being called a “candidate planet” for now, until further analysis or observation confirms its status. Researchers believe WD1856b likely didn’t form at its current location. According to the study team’s calculations, the candidate planet must have come into being about 50 times further away from the star than its current location.
